Conway's Game of Life

Welcome to Conway's Game of Life, a fascinating cellular automaton created by mathematician John Conway in 1970. Despite being called a "game," it's actually a zero-player game where evolution is determined by its initial state.

The Rules

The universe consists of a grid of cells, each of which can be either alive or dead. Every generation, each cell's fate is determined by these simple rules:

  • Survival: A living cell with 2 or 3 living neighbors survives
  • Birth: A dead cell with exactly 3 living neighbors becomes alive
  • Death: All other cells die from loneliness or overcrowding

How to Play

  • Click cells to toggle them between alive and dead
  • Press Play to start the simulation
  • Use presets to explore famous patterns like gliders and oscillators
  • Adjust speed to watch evolution at your preferred pace
  • Step through generations one at a time for detailed observation
  • Export patterns to save your creations as JSON files
  • Import patterns to load saved configurations or community patterns

Keyboard Shortcuts

  • Spacebar: Play/Pause
  • S: Step forward one generation
  • R: Reset the grid
  • G: Generate random pattern
  • Ctrl/Cmd + E: Export current pattern
  • Ctrl/Cmd + I: Import pattern from file
  • Escape: Close modals

Ambient Experience

Toggle the music control in the bottom-right corner for a relaxing ambient soundtrack while you explore the emergence of complex patterns from simple rules.
